Variance: Definition & Proof

A quick reference for POM Tutorial 3 — Managing Risks (Newsvendor I)

1. Definition

The variance of a random variable \(X\) is the expected value of the squared deviation from the mean \(\mu = E[X]\):

\[ \operatorname{Var}(X) = E\!\left[(X - \mu)^2\right] \]

This definition works for discrete, continuous, or mixed random variables. The variance can also be written as the covariance of \(X\) with itself:

\[ \operatorname{Var}(X) = \operatorname{Cov}(X, X) \]

Common notation: \(\operatorname{Var}(X)\), \(V(X)\), \(\sigma_X^2\), or simply \(\sigma^2\) (pronounced "sigma squared").

2. Proof: The Shortcut Formula

The variance can be expanded into a simpler computational form:

Claim: \(\operatorname{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2\)

Proof: Starting from the definition:

\[ \begin{aligned} \operatorname{Var}(X) &= E\!\left[(X - E[X])^2\right] \\[6pt] &= E\!\left[X^2 - 2X \cdot E[X] + (E[X])^2\right] \\[6pt] &= E[X^2] - 2\,E[X] \cdot E[X] + (E[X])^2 \\[6pt] &= E[X^2] - 2\,(E[X])^2 + (E[X])^2 \\[6pt] &= E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 \end{aligned} \]

In words: the variance equals the mean of the square minus the square of the mean.

3. Discrete Random Variables

If \(X\) takes values \(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\) with probabilities \(p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_n\), then:

\[ \mu = E[X] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i \cdot x_i \]
\[ \operatorname{Var}(X) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i \cdot (x_i - \mu)^2 \]

Or equivalently, using the shortcut formula:

\[ \sigma^2 = \left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i \cdot x_i^2\right) - \mu^2 \]

4. Standard Deviation

The standard deviation is simply the square root of the variance:

\[ \sigma = \sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(X)} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i \cdot (x_i - \mu)^2} \]

5. Connecting to the Newsvendor: From Discrete to Normal

Why this matters for the Newsvendor problem:

In Tutorial 3, you often start with a discrete demand distribution (e.g., Matt Herron's overcoats: 100, 200, 300, 400 with given probabilities) and need to find the "equivalent" normal distribution for the normal approximation method.

Step-by-step: Discrete → Normal

Given: A discrete demand distribution with values \(d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n\) and probabilities \(p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_n\).

Step 1: Compute the mean \(\mu\)

\[ \mu = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i \cdot d_i \]

Step 2: Compute the variance \(\sigma^2\)

\[ \sigma^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i \cdot (d_i - \mu)^2 \]

Step 3: Take the square root to get \(\sigma\)

\[ \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2} \]

Step 4: The "equivalent" normal distribution is \(D \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\)

You can now use the normal Newsvendor formula: \(Q^* = \mu + z^* \cdot \sigma\), where \(\Phi(z^*) = CR\).

\(\sigma = \sqrt{6{,}500} = 80.6\), so the equivalent normal distribution is \(D \sim N(250, \, 80.6^2)\).

Important note: The normal approximation works best when the discrete distribution is roughly symmetric and bell-shaped. It can be inaccurate when:
  • The distribution is highly skewed
  • There are very few discrete values
  • \(\sigma\) is large relative to \(\mu\) (the normal can assign probability to negative demand)

When in doubt, use the discrete method (CDF table with \(F(Q) \geq CR\)) for exact results, and the normal method for quick estimates or when only \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) are given.
